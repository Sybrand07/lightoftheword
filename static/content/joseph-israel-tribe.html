Genesis 48: Why Was Joseph Not a Tribe of Israel?

Joseph, despite being one of Jacob’s sons, did not have a tribe named after him. Instead, his two sons, Ephraim and Manasseh, each became their own tribe. This was because:

  • Jacob Adopted Ephraim and Manasseh as His Own Sons (Genesis 48:5-6)
    • Jacob explicitly says, “Now your two sons, who were born to you in the land of Egypt before I came to you in Egypt, are mine; Ephraim and Manasseh shall be mine, as Reuben and Simeon are.”
    • This meant that Joseph’s descendants would receive a double portion of inheritance in the Promised Land.
  • The Firstborn’s Double Portion
    • In Israelite tradition, the firstborn received a double portion of the inheritance (Deuteronomy 21:17).
    • Although Joseph was not Jacob’s firstborn, he was the firstborn of Rachel, Jacob’s favored wife. By giving Joseph’s portion to Ephraim and Manasseh, Jacob essentially gave Joseph the firstborn’s blessing.
  • Tribal Distribution in Israel
    • Instead of one "Tribe of Joseph," Israel had the Tribe of Ephraim and the Tribe of Manasseh.
    • This made up for the absence of a Joseph-named tribe and maintained 12 tribal divisions in the Promised Land.

2. Why Did Jacob Not Recognize the Two Boys?

  • Jacob’s Failing Eyesight
    • The text says, “Now the eyes of Israel were dim with age, so that he could not see” (Genesis 48:10).
    • This parallels the situation with Isaac, who was blind when blessing Jacob and Esau (Genesis 27).
    • While he knew of Joseph’s sons, he may not have been able to clearly see them.
  • Egyptian Upbringing & Appearance
    • Ephraim and Manasseh were born and raised in Egypt, likely dressed as Egyptian nobility and possibly looked different from the Hebrew family.
    • They may have had Egyptian hairstyles and clothing, making them unrecognizable to Jacob at first.
  • A Formal Ritual Question
    • Some scholars believe Jacob’s question, “Who are these?” (Genesis 48:8) wasn’t just about recognition.
    • It may have been a formal way of initiating a blessing ceremony, confirming their identity before adopting them.

3. How Did the Blessing on Ephraim Over Manasseh Play Out in History?

Jacob crossed his hands and gave the greater blessing to Ephraim, though he was younger. Joseph protested, but Jacob insisted that Ephraim would be greater. This prophecy was fulfilled in Israel’s history.

A. Ephraim Became the Dominant Tribe in Israel

  • Manasseh Was Numerous but Less Influential
    • Manasseh became a large tribe (even split into two, with land on both sides of the Jordan River).
    • However, it did not hold as much political or spiritual influence as Ephraim.
  • Ephraim Became the Leading Tribe in the Northern Kingdom
    • During the period of the Judges and the early monarchy, Ephraim was one of the most powerful tribes.
    • Many judges, like Joshua (Numbers 13:8, Deuteronomy 34:9), were from Ephraim.
    • When Israel split into two kingdoms (after Solomon’s death), Ephraim became the dominant tribe of the Northern Kingdom (Israel).
    • So much so that the entire Northern Kingdom was sometimes called "Ephraim" in prophetic writings (e.g., Isaiah 7:9, Hosea 5:3).

B. Manasseh Was Large but Divided

  • Manasseh was split into two half-tribes (one on each side of the Jordan River).
  • This geographical split led to less unity and influence.
  • While important, it never overshadowed Ephraim's dominance.

C. Decline of Ephraim

  • Despite its dominance, Ephraim later fell into idolatry and was rebuked by the prophets (Hosea 4:17).
  • The Northern Kingdom (which included Ephraim) was conquered by Assyria in 722 BC, leading to its dispersal and assimilation (the so-called "Lost Tribes of Israel").
  • Over time, Judah (the Southern Kingdom) became the leading tribe, eventually giving rise to King David and later Jesus Christ.

Conclusion

Jacob’s blessing over Ephraim and Manasseh shaped Israel’s history. Ephraim became the dominant tribe, fulfilling Jacob’s prophecy, while Manasseh was large but not as influential. Joseph did not have a tribe named after him because his inheritance was given through his two sons, ensuring a double portion of blessing.
