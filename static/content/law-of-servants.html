Exodus 21: Understanding the Law of Servants

Exodus 21, part of the Covenant Code in the Torah, addresses laws related to servitude, violence, property rights, and personal injury. The chapter begins by outlining the treatment and regulation of Hebrew servants.

1. The Law of Hebrew Servants (Exodus 21:1-11)

  1. Duration of Servitude (Exodus 21:2-4):
    • A Hebrew servant was to serve for six years and be set free in the seventh year without paying anything.
    • If he came in single, he would leave single; if married, his wife would go with him.
    • If the master gave him a wife and they had children, the wife and children remained with the master. The servant could choose to remain with his family by voluntarily continuing his service.
  2. Permanent Servitude (Exodus 21:5-6):
    • If the servant declared love for his master and family and wished to stay, his master would pierce his ear as a sign of lifelong service.
  3. Female Servants (Exodus 21:7-11):
    • A daughter sold as a maidservant was not to be set free as male servants were. This was more of a marital arrangement than servitude.
    • If her master was displeased with her (did not marry her or treat her as a wife), she was to be redeemed (freed or bought back).
    • She was to be treated with dignity and was not to be sold to foreigners. If given to the master's son, she was to be treated as a daughter.
    • If the master took another wife, he must not diminish her food, clothing, or marital rights. Failure to uphold these would grant her freedom without payment.

 

Historical Context:

  1. Societal Structure:
    • The laws in Exodus 21 were given to a recently freed Israelite people who had been slaves in Egypt. These regulations addressed a transitional society moving from slavery to a covenant community.
  2. Economic Realities:
    • In ancient times, servitude often arose from debt or poverty. A man might sell himself into servitude to pay off debts or secure a livelihood.
  3. Protection and Dignity:
    • Unlike harsh slavery systems seen in other ancient cultures, Hebrew servitude was regulated and humane. The laws emphasized justice and protection, particularly for vulnerable servants.
  4. Voluntary Bondage:
    • A servant choosing lifelong service was treated with respect and could not be coerced. The ear-piercing ritual symbolized commitment rather than ownership.

 

Why Did God Ordain This?

  1. Regulating an Existing System:
    • God did not create slavery but regulated it within the context of human brokenness. These laws aimed to limit abuse and provide dignity, reflecting God’s justice and mercy.
  2. Preserving Social Structure:
    • As Israel moved from slavery to freedom, servitude laws allowed for economic stability while maintaining human dignity.
  3. Moral Development:
    • These laws served as a step toward higher moral standards. Over time, the prophetic literature would increasingly denounce oppression and advocate for social justice.

 

Slavery Post-Jesus: A New Perspective

  1. The Gospel's Impact:
    • Jesus’ teachings and the New Testament writings significantly transformed attitudes towards servitude:
      • Galatians 3:28: “There is neither slave nor free... for you are all one in Christ Jesus.”
      • Philemon: Paul urges Philemon to receive his servant Onesimus “no longer as a servant, but more than a servant, a beloved brother.”
  2. Transformation of Relationships:
    • Jesus called for servant leadership (Mark 10:44) and mutual love, leading to the abolitionist mindset that Christians would later champion.
  3. Theological Shift:
    • The early church, under Paul’s teaching, moved towards recognizing the dignity of every individual. Slavery was increasingly seen as incompatible with the brotherhood of believers.
  4. Modern Reflection:
    • In the light of the Gospel, slavery is seen as inherently incompatible with Christian values. The goal became freedom, equality, and love for all, in contrast to the temporary and regulated servitude in the Old Testament.

 

Conclusion:

Exodus 21 reflects God's desire to protect and regulate a social practice common in ancient times, emphasizing human dignity and justice. With the advent of Christ, the emphasis shifted from regulation to transformation, promoting equality and love as the foundation of human relationships.
