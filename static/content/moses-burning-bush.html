Exodus 3: Moses' Encounter with God

Exodus 3 is a foundational passage in biblical theology, introducing Moses’ divine calling, the concept of sacred space (holy ground), and the revelation of God’s name, "I AM WHO I AM" (YHWH). This chapter lays the groundwork for understanding God’s transcendence, immanence, holiness, and covenantal faithfulness.

 

1. Moses' Encounter with God (Exodus 3:1–6)

The Setting: Wilderness and Mount Horeb (Sinai)

  • Moses, a former prince of Egypt, is now a shepherd in the wilderness of Midian, where he has spent 40 years (Acts 7:30).
  • The location, Mount Horeb (Sinai), will later become the site of the giving of the Law (Torah) (Exodus 19–20).
  • Biblically, the wilderness often symbolizes a place of testing, purification, and divine encounters (e.g., Israel's wandering, Jesus’ temptation in the desert).

The Burning Bush: Theophany of God's Presence

  • “The angel of the LORD appeared to him in flames of fire from within a bush” (Exodus 3:2).
  • Fire is a common biblical symbol of God’s presence (e.g., the pillar of fire in Exodus 13:21, the fire on Mount Sinai in Exodus 19:18, the fire of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost in Acts 2:3).
  • The bush is burning but not consumed, indicating:
    • God’s self-sustaining nature (He is not dependent on creation).
    • God’s purifying presence (fire refines but does not destroy His chosen ones).
    • Israel’s suffering (afflicted but not consumed by Egypt).

God Calls Moses: The Personal, Covenant-Keeping God

  • God calls Moses by name ("Moses, Moses!"), showing personal knowledge and divine election.
  • Moses' response, “Here I am,” echoes Abraham’s response to God in Genesis 22:1.
  • The repetition of Moses' name signals intimacy and urgency in divine summons.

 

2. Holy Ground: The Sacredness of God's Presence (Exodus 3:5-6)

"Do Not Come Any Closer" – The Holiness of God

  • God instructs Moses: “Take off your sandals, for the place where you are standing is holy ground.”
  • This is the first explicit mention of "holy ground" in Scripture, emphasizing:
    • Holiness as separation (God is utterly distinct from creation).
    • God’s presence sanctifies—the ground itself is not inherently holy, but it becomes so due to God’s manifest presence.
    • The necessity of reverence in approaching God (later seen in Levitical worship).

Removing Sandals: Symbol of Humility and Unworthiness

  • In ancient Near Eastern culture, removing sandals signified respect, submission, and purity.
  • This foreshadows later priestly requirements: the high priest must approach God with reverence (Leviticus 16:4).
  • It also recalls Jesus washing His disciples' feet (John 13), signifying humility before God.

God Identifies Himself: The God of the Patriarchs

  • “I am the God of your father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob” (Exodus 3:6).
  • This reaffirms God’s covenant faithfulness (Genesis 12:1–3, 15:13–16).
  • By linking Himself to the patriarchs, God assures Moses that He remembers His promises to Israel.
  • Moses hides his face, overwhelmed by God’s holiness and transcendence—a common response to divine encounters (e.g., Isaiah 6:5, Revelation 1:17).

 

3. God's Commission: The Deliverance of Israel (Exodus 3:7–12)

God’s Compassion and Promise of Deliverance

  • “I have seen... I have heard... I know...” (Exodus 3:7).
  • Unlike the distant deities of pagan religions, the God of Israel is personally involved with His people’s suffering.
  • God promises to deliver Israel from Egypt and bring them to a land flowing with milk and honey (Canaan).
  • This deliverance fulfills the covenant with Abraham (Genesis 15:13–16).

Moses' Reluctance and God's Reassurance

  • Moses objects: “Who am I that I should go to Pharaoh?” (Exodus 3:11).
  • God’s response: “I will be with you.”
  • This shifts the focus from Moses' ability to God’s sufficiency—a key biblical theme (e.g., 2 Corinthians 12:9).
  • The sign of God's promise is future: “When you have brought the people out of Egypt, you will worship God on this mountain.”

 

4. God's Name Revealed: "I AM WHO I AM" (Exodus 3:13–15)

Moses’ Question: “What is His Name?”

  • In the ancient world, knowing a deity’s name implied understanding their nature and having access to them.
  • Moses anticipates that the Israelites will ask, “What is His name?”—seeking to understand God’s identity.

God's Response: "I AM WHO I AM" (YHWH, יהוה)

  • God declares: “I AM WHO I AM” (ehyeh asher ehyeh, אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה) (Exodus 3:14).
  • This phrase carries profound theological significance:
    1. God’s Self-Existence (Aseity): God does not derive existence from anything else; He is uncreated.
    2. God’s Eternality: God exists outside of time—past, present, and future are one before Him.
    3. God’s Unchangeability (Immutability): Unlike shifting human realities, God is constant and faithful (Malachi 3:6).
    4. God’s Active Presence: “I AM” signifies that God is actively involved in history and will be with His people (Matthew 28:20).

The Covenant Name: YHWH (יהוה)

  • God then declares: “Say to the Israelites: ‘I AM has sent me to you.’”
  • He further clarifies: “This is my name forever, the name you shall call me from generation to generation.”
  • The name YHWH (Yahweh), derived from “I AM,” becomes Israel’s covenant name for God.
  • This name was considered so sacred that later Jews would substitute Adonai (Lord) in reading it aloud.

 

Theological Implications of Exodus 3

  1. God’s Transcendence and Immanence
    • God is above creation (transcendent), yet He is personally involved (immanent).
  2. The Call to Holiness
    • Approaching God requires reverence and purity.
  3. God’s Faithfulness to His Covenant
    • The promise to Abraham is being fulfilled—God remembers His people.
  4. Divine Presence as the Basis of Mission
    • Moses is not sent in his own strength, but with God’s presence (“I will be with you”).
  5. The Revelation of God’s Name (YHWH)
    • God is self-existent, eternal, unchanging, and actively present with His people.
  6. A Foreshadowing of Christ
    • Jesus later declares, “Before Abraham was, I AM” (John 8:58)—identifying Himself with the God of Exodus 3.

This encounter marks the foundation of Israel’s identity and shapes the biblical understanding of God as holy, present, and faithful to His promises.
