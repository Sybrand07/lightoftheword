1 John 3:8: Sin or Sinning?

1 John 3:8 (NIV) states:
"The one who does what is sinful is of the devil, because the devil has been sinning from the beginning. The reason the Son of God appeared was to destroy the devil's work."

This verse highlights the purpose of Jesus Christ's mission on earth: to overcome sin and the power of the devil, breaking the hold of evil and setting people free to live in righteousness. It emphasizes the transformative power of Christ's victory over sin.

The distinction between "sin" and "keep sinning" in 1 John 3:8 depends on the translation and the interpretation of the original Greek text. The verb used, "ποιεῖ" (poiei), is in the present tense, which often conveys a continuous or habitual action in Greek.

Key Differences in Translations:

  1. NIV (keep sinning):
    • "The one who does what is sinful is of the devil."
    • Suggests a lifestyle or ongoing practice of sin rather than a single act.
  2. ESV (practices sin):
    • "Whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil."
    • Emphasizes habitual sinning as a way of life.
  3. KJV (committeth sin):
    • "He that committeth sin is of the devil."
    • May sound like it refers to any sin but is interpreted by scholars to align with ongoing action.

Theological Understanding:

  • "Keeps sinning" or "practices sin" aligns with the context of 1 John 3, which contrasts a life characterized by sin versus one transformed by Christ.
  • The emphasis is on unrepentant, habitual sin as evidence of alignment with the devil, while a person born of God (verse 9) does not "practice" sin as a way of life.

In summary, the original Greek supports the idea of habitual sin rather than a single act, reinforcing the transformative nature of a life in Christ that moves away from persistent sin.
