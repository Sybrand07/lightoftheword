Exodus 2: Who Did the Priest of Midian Worship?

In Exodus 2, the priest of Midian is introduced when Moses flees Egypt and arrives in Midian. This priest, later identified as Jethro (also called Reuel), had seven daughters who were tending to their father’s flock. When shepherds tried to drive them away from a well, Moses intervened and helped them water their sheep. As a result, their father invited Moses to his home, and eventually, Moses married his daughter Zipporah (Exodus 2:16-22).

The Midianites were descendants of Midian, a son of Abraham and Keturah (Genesis 25:1-2). Because of this Abrahamic lineage, they may have had some knowledge of Yahweh (the God of Israel). However, historical and biblical evidence suggests that their religious practices were mixed and included elements of polytheism.

1. Midianite Worship and Yahweh

  • Some scholars believe that the Midianites had a connection to Yahweh-worship before Moses' time. This is because Moses first encounters God at Mount Horeb (Sinai) in Midian (Exodus 3:1-6).
  • Later, Jethro (the priest of Midian) acknowledges Yahweh's supremacy in Exodus 18:11, saying:

“Now I know that the Lord (Yahweh) is greater than all gods.”
This suggests he may have believed in multiple gods but recognized Yahweh as the highest.

  • Some scholars propose that the Kenite Hypothesis (based on Judges 1:16) suggests that Yahweh-worship may have originated from the Midianites or Kenites and was later adopted by Israel.

2. Midianite Polytheism

  • The Midianites, like many ancient Semitic tribes, likely worshiped multiple gods alongside Yahweh.
  • Later in Numbers 25, the Israelites are led into idolatry by Midianite women, worshiping Baal of Peor—a Canaanite deity. This indicates that Midianites were not exclusive Yahweh-worshippers.
  • Archaeological evidence suggests the Midianites also worshiped a deity named Qos (or Quos), who was possibly a storm or warrior god.

3. Was Jethro a Priest of Yahweh?

The Bible calls Jethro a "priest of Midian" (Exodus 2:16), but it does not specify which god(s) he served.

  • If he was a priest of Yahweh, this would support the idea that some Midianites retained true worship.
  • If he was a priest of other gods, then his recognition of Yahweh in Exodus 18 suggests a conversion moment.

Conclusion

Jethro, as a priest of Midian, may have had knowledge of Yahweh but likely also engaged in Midianite religious practices. By Exodus 18, he recognizes Yahweh as the supreme God, though the Midianites as a whole continued to worship other deities, such as Baal and Qos.
