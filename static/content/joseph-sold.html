Genesis 37: A Play-by-Play of Joseph Being Sold by His Brothers

This passage describes the pivotal event in Joseph's life—his betrayal by his brothers, leading to his eventual journey to Egypt. Below is a verse-by-verse analysis with an exploration of the significance of each detail.

Verses 12-17: Joseph Sent to His Brothers

12. "Then his brothers went to pasture their father's flock near Shechem."

  • This reflects their role as shepherds and reminds us of the earlier tragedy at Shechem (Genesis 34), where Simeon and Levi massacred the Shechemites. Their return to this location suggests that Jacob still held land there.

13. "And Israel said to Joseph, 'Are not your brothers pasturing the flock at Shechem? Come, I will send you to them.'"

  • Jacob (here called "Israel") trusts Joseph, showing his favored position.
  • The phrase "I will send you" foreshadows God's greater sending of Joseph to Egypt.

14. "So he said to him, 'Go now, see if it is well with your brothers and with the flock, and bring me word.' So he sent him from the Valley of Hebron, and he came to Shechem."

  • "Hebron" (meaning fellowship) contrasts with Shechem, a place of past bloodshed.
  • Joseph's mission foreshadows Christ's mission, being sent by the Father to his "brethren" (John 1:11).

15. "And a man found him wandering in the fields. And the man asked him, 'What are you seeking?'"

  • This unnamed man serves as a divine guide directing Joseph.
  • Symbolically, Joseph "seeks" his brothers, just as Christ seeks the lost (Luke 19:10).

16. "I am seeking my brothers," he said. "Tell me, please, where they are pasturing the flock."

  • This reinforces Joseph's innocence—he seeks his brothers in good faith.
  • Again, this parallels Christ's love for His people.

17. "And the man said, 'They have gone away, for I heard them say, "Let us go to Dothan."' So Joseph went after his brothers and found them at Dothan."

  • Dothan (meaning "two wells") is about 20 miles north of Shechem.
  • This change in location plays a key role—it places Joseph near the major trade routes to Egypt.

Verses 18-24: The Brothers— Conspiracy and Joseph Thrown into a Pit

18. "They saw him from afar, and before he came near to them they conspired against him to kill him."

  • The phrase —saw him from afar— suggests his distinctive coat was visible.
  • Their premeditated conspiracy highlights their deep jealousy and resentment.

19. "They said to one another, 'Here comes this dreamer!'"

  • This mocking title shows their bitterness toward Joseph's prophetic dreams.
  • Rejecting Joseph's dreams is symbolically rejecting God's plan.

20. "Come now, let us kill him and throw him into one of the pits. Then we will say that a fierce animal has devoured him, and we will see what will become of his dreams."

  • Their intent to kill mirrors Cain's jealousy of Abel (Genesis 4:8).
  • They believe they can thwart God's plan, but this irony is evident: their actions actually fulfill Joseph's dreams.

21-22. "But when Reuben heard it, he rescued him out of their hands, saying, 'Let us not take his life.' And Reuben said to them, 'Shed no blood; throw him into this pit here in the wilderness, but do not lay a hand on him'—that he might rescue him out of their hand to restore him to his father."

  • Reuben, as the eldest, seeks to prevent murder but lacks the courage to openly oppose them.
  • His plan to "rescue" Joseph later ultimately fails, highlighting his weak leadership.

23. "So when Joseph came to his brothers, they stripped him of his robe, the robe of many colors that he wore."

  • Stripping his robe represents stripping him of his status and favor.
  • This foreshadows Christ being stripped of His garments before crucifixion (Matthew 27:28).

24. "And they took him and threw him into a pit. The pit was empty; there was no water in it."

  • The pit (probably a dry cistern) symbolizes death and abandonment.
  • It parallels Christ's death and burial.

Verses 25-28: Joseph Sold into Slavery

25. "Then they sat down to eat. And looking up they saw a caravan of Ishmaelites coming from Gilead, with their camels bearing gum, balm, and myrrh, on their way to carry it down to Egypt."

  • Sitting to eat while Joseph suffers shows their hardened hearts.
  • The caravan's trade items—gum, balm, and myrrh—are significant:
    • Balm (healing ointment) ironically foreshadows Joseph's role in Egypt, where he will later save lives.
    • Myrrh was later used for embalming, pointing to Christ's burial.

26-27. "Then Judah said to his brothers, 'What profit is it if we kill our brother and conceal his blood? Come, let us sell him to the Ishmaelites, and let not our hand be upon him, for he is our brother, our own flesh.' And his brothers listened to him."

  • Judah (ancestor of Jesus) suggests selling Joseph, prefiguring Judas Iscariot's betrayal of Christ for silver (Matthew 26:14-16).

28. "Then Midianite traders passed by, and they drew Joseph up and lifted him out of the pit and sold him to the Ishmaelites for twenty shekels of silver. They took Joseph to Egypt."

  • Twenty shekels was the price of a young slave (Leviticus 27:5).
  • Like Jesus, Joseph was "sold for silver" and handed over to Gentiles.

Verses 29-36: Deception and Jacob's Grief

29-30. "When Reuben returned to the pit and saw that Joseph was not in the pit, he tore his clothes. He returned to his brothers and said, 'The boy is gone, and I, where shall I go?'"

  • Tearing clothes symbolizes deep mourning and loss.
  • Reuben fears his father's wrath, showing his concern for himself more than Joseph.

31-32. "Then they took Joseph's robe and slaughtered a goat and dipped the robe in the blood. And they sent the robe of many colors and brought it to their father and said, 'This we have found; please identify whether it is your son's robe or not.'"

  • This deception using a goat's blood mirrors Jacob's own deception of Isaac (Genesis 27).
  • Jacob is now deceived just as he deceived his father.

33-34. "And he identified it and said, 'It is my son's robe. A fierce animal has devoured him. Joseph is without doubt torn to pieces.' Then Jacob tore his garments and put sackcloth on his loins and mourned for his son many days."

  • Jacob's grief is genuine, but his belief is based on deception.

35-36. "All his sons and all his daughters rose up to comfort him, but he refused to be comforted— Meanwhile, the Midianites had sold him in Egypt to Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh, the captain of the guard."

  • The contrast is striking—Jacob mourns as if all is lost, while Joseph is actually being positioned for his divine destiny in Egypt.

Theological Significance

  • Foreshadowing of Christ: Joseph's betrayal, suffering, and eventual rise mirror Jesus' death, burial, and resurrection.
  • God's Sovereignty: What the brothers intended for evil, God used for good (Genesis 50:20).
  • Human Sin and Redemption: The brothers' actions are driven by jealousy, but redemption is ultimately achieved through Joseph.

This passage is a turning point in salvation history, setting the stage for Israel's future in Egypt and God's grand plan of redemption.
