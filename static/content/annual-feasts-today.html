Exodus 23: How Do Christians Commemorate the Feasts Today?

Let’s delve into Exodus 23:14-19, examining its original context, spiritual significance, and how it relates to Christian practices today. We’ll also address the timing of the feasts, how offerings were made, and whether they were consumed or burnt.

Exodus 23:14-19 (ESV)

14 "Three times in the year you shall keep a feast to me.
15 You shall keep the Feast of Unleavened Bread. As I commanded you, you shall eat unleavened bread for seven days at the appointed time in the month of Abib, for in it you came out of Egypt. None shall appear before me empty-handed.
16 You shall keep the Feast of Harvest, of the firstfruits of your labor, of what you sow in the field. You shall keep the Feast of Ingathering at the end of the year, when you gather in from the field the fruit of your labor.
17 Three times in the year shall all your males appear before the Lord God.
18 You shall not offer the blood of my sacrifice with anything leavened, or let the fat of my feast remain until the morning.
19 The best of the firstfruits of your ground you shall bring into the house of the Lord your God. You shall not boil a young goat in its mother's milk."

a) Feast of Unleavened Bread (Passover)

Ancient Practice:

  • Celebrated annually for seven days (Exodus 12:15).
  • The Israelites ate unleavened bread to commemorate leaving Egypt in haste.
  • Sacrifice: The Passover lamb was sacrificed, its blood marking the doorposts. The lamb was roasted and consumed.

Christian Perspective:

  • Fulfillment in Christ: Jesus is the Passover Lamb (1 Corinthians 5:7). His sacrifice delivers believers from the slavery of sin.
  • Modern Practice: Christians celebrate Easter, reflecting on Christ’s death and resurrection.
  • Spiritual Application: Removing the “leaven” of sin through repentance and self-examination.
  • Example: Observing communion as a reminder of Jesus’ sacrifice.

 

b) Feast of Harvest (Firstfruits/Pentecost)

Ancient Practice:

  • Marked the beginning of the wheat harvest.
  • Offerings of the firstfruits were brought to the Temple and consumed by priests.
  • Burnt or Consumed: Grain offerings were generally not burnt entirely; some were eaten by the priests after a portion was burnt as a memorial.

Christian Perspective:

  • Fulfillment in Pentecost: The Holy Spirit descended on the disciples (Acts 2).
  • Modern Practice: Celebrating Pentecost as the birth of the Church and the empowerment of believers.
  • Spiritual Application: Dedicate the “first” of anything to God—income, time, or talents.
  • Example: Tithing the first portion of your salary or dedicating the first hours of your day to prayer.

 

c) Feast of Ingathering (Tabernacles)

Ancient Practice:

  • Celebrated at the end of the agricultural year as a thanksgiving for the final harvest.
  • The people lived in booths (temporary shelters) to remember their wilderness journey.
  • Burnt or Consumed: Offerings included burnt offerings and portions consumed as communal meals.

Christian Perspective:

  • Symbol of God’s Provision: Points to the ultimate gathering of believers in God’s Kingdom (Revelation 21:3).
  • Modern Practice: Thanksgiving services or harvest festivals.
  • Spiritual Application: Reflecting on God’s provision and expressing gratitude.
  • Example: Hosting a family thanksgiving dinner where everyone shares what they are grateful for.

 

2. Requirements and Restrictions (Verses 18-19)

a) Blood with Leaven (v. 18)

  • Ancient Practice: Blood sacrifices represented purification and atonement. Mixing them with leaven (symbolizing sin) was prohibited.
  • Spiritual Significance:
    • Holiness in Worship: Offering a pure heart, free from hypocrisy and sin.
  • Christian Application: Approach God with repentance and purity.
  • Example: Confessing sins before taking communion to maintain spiritual integrity (1 Corinthians 11:28).

 

b) Fat Not Left Until Morning (v. 18)

  • Ancient Practice: The fat was the best part, signifying richness and abundance, and was to be burned immediately as an offering.
  • Spiritual Significance:
    • Promptness in Offering: Delayed obedience diminishes the value of the sacrifice.
  • Christian Application: Give to God without hesitation or delay.
  • Example: Prioritizing giving to God first before spending on personal desires.

 

c) Best of Firstfruits (v. 19)

  • Ancient Practice: Bringing the best of the first harvest to the Temple as a sign of honor to God.
  • Burnt or Consumed: Some offerings were burnt, while others (like bread) were shared or eaten.
  • Spiritual Significance:
    • Excellence in Worship: Offering God the best, not leftovers.
  • Christian Application: Serve God with excellence in all areas of life.
  • Example: Volunteering your skills at church rather than just your spare time.

 

d) Not Boiling a Young Goat in Its Mother’s Milk (v. 19)

  • Ancient Practice: Likely a prohibition against a Canaanite ritual, symbolizing the separation of life and death.
  • Spiritual Significance:
    • Compassion and Distinction: Do not mix what God intended to be separate.
  • Christian Application: Maintain moral and ethical integrity by avoiding practices rooted in ungodly traditions.
  • Example: Evaluating cultural customs through the lens of Scripture.

 

Practical Lessons for Christians Today

  1. Regular Reflection and Gratitude: Just as the Israelites had specific seasons for thanksgiving, Christians should set aside time to thank God for His blessings.
  2. First and Best for God: Always give your best efforts, time, and resources in worship and service.
  3. Holiness and Integrity: Worship should be pure, without a mixture of sin or compromise.
  4. Prioritize Offering: Do not delay when it comes to giving your best to God.
  5. Cultural Discernment: Avoid blending Christian practices with ungodly customs.

By reflecting on the ancient practices of the Israelites and their transformation through Christ, Christians can better understand how to live out these principles today.
