Genesis 39: What was the Accusation of Potiphar’s Wife’s Against Joseph?

In Genesis 39, Joseph, who had been sold into slavery by his brothers, was serving in the house of Potiphar, an Egyptian official. Because of Joseph’s integrity and God's favor, he was put in charge of Potiphar’s entire household. However, Potiphar’s wife persistently attempted to seduce him. Joseph consistently refused, saying:

"How then can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" (Genesis 39:9, KJV)

Eventually, when she grabbed his garment, Joseph fled, leaving his cloak behind. She then falsely accused him to cover her own shame and anger at being rejected.

The Accusation in Genesis 39:14-18

Potiphar's wife twisted the truth and accused Joseph of attempting to rape her. She framed him in two ways:

1.        First, she called the household servants and falsely claimed that Joseph had come to mock them (or her).

2.      Later, she told Potiphar the same accusation, emphasizing that Joseph had left his garment behind, making it appear as evidence of his guilt.

Here is what she said in Genesis 39:14-15 (ESV):

"She called to the men of her household and said to them, 'See, he has brought among us a Hebrew to laugh at us. He came in to lie with me, and I cried out with a loud voice. And as soon as he heard that I lifted up my voice and cried out, he left his garment beside me and fled and got out of the house.'”

Key Hebrew Words in the Accusation

1.        "צָחַק" (ṣāḥaq) – "Mock, Laugh, Play"

o    In Genesis 39:14, Potiphar’s wife accused Joseph of coming to "mock" them:

"See, he has brought among us a Hebrew to laugh at us" (וַיְהִי כְהַרְאֹתִי בְקוֹלִי לִזְעֹק וַיַּעֲזֹב בִּגְדּוֹ אֶצְלִי וַיָּנָס וַיֵּצֵא הַחוּצָה׃)"

o    The word ṣāḥaq is ambiguous. While it can mean literal laughter, it is also used in sexual connotations elsewhere in the Bible.

2.      Where Else is צָחַק (ṣāḥaq) Used?

o    Genesis 21:9 – Ishmael was seen "mocking" or "playing" with Isaac, which led to Sarah demanding his expulsion.

o    Exodus 32:6 – The Israelites "rose up to play" (ṣāḥaq), referring to idolatrous revelry and likely immoral behavior.

o    Judges 16:25 – The Philistines brought out Samson to "entertain" them, using the same verb.

This suggests Potiphar’s wife might have implied that Joseph tried to take advantage of her sexually or humiliate her.

3.      "שָׁכַב" (shāḵaḇ) – "Lie with" (Sexual Connotation)

o    In Genesis 39:14-15, 18, she says Joseph "came in to lie with me" (לִשְׁכַּב עִמִּי).

o    This phrase is commonly used in the Bible to describe sexual relations (whether consensual or forced).

o    Example: Genesis 34:2 – Shechem "lay with" (shāḵaḇ) Dinah, referring to her being violated.

The Consequences of the False Accusation

·         Potiphar, though furious, did not have Joseph executed (which would have been expected for an actual assault attempt).

·         Instead, Joseph was imprisoned (Genesis 39:20), suggesting Potiphar may have doubted his wife’s story.

·         Even in prison, God remained with Joseph, ultimately leading to his rise to power in Egypt (Genesis 41).

Final Thoughts

Potiphar’s wife’s accusation was not just about a false charge of rape, but also about racial prejudice ("this Hebrew"), manipulating emotions, and distorting truth. The Hebrew word "ṣāḥaq" (to mock/play/laugh) has deeper implications, often tied to immoral behavior, making her claim seem even more deceitful.
