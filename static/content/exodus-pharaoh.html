Exodus 1–14: Evidence for the Biblical Pharoah

The historical identification of the Pharaoh of the Exodus is a debated topic, with multiple theories regarding the timeline and the specific ruler involved. However, a strong argument exists for Thutmose III (d. 1450 BC) as the Pharaoh of the oppression and Amenhotep II as the Pharaoh of the Exodus. Below is a detailed exposition of this perspective, covering the key figures, archaeological evidence, chronology, and related biblical connections.

 

1. The Pharaoh of the Oppression: Thutmose III (Reigned 1479–1425 BC)

Thutmose III is often considered the Pharaoh who oppressed the Israelites before the Exodus. His long and militarily aggressive reign makes him a fitting candidate.

Evidence Supporting Thutmose III as the Pharaoh of the Oppression:

  • Lengthy Reign: He ruled for 54 years, first as co-regent with Hatshepsut (his stepmother/aunt) and later as sole ruler. His long reign allowed for the oppression of the Israelites to be prolonged.
  • Imperial Expansion: He conducted 17 military campaigns, making Egypt one of the most powerful empires of the time.
  • Massive Construction Projects: His reign saw many construction efforts that may have required forced labor, such as the rebuilding of temples in Karnak and Luxor.

His Death and Possible Connection to Moses' Return

  • Thutmose III died around 1450 BC, which aligns with the biblical narrative of Moses returning to Egypt after the Pharaoh who sought his life (Exodus 4:19) had died.
  • This date fits well with the traditional biblical chronology of the Exodus occurring around 1446 BC (1 Kings 6:1 states the Exodus happened 480 years before the construction of Solomon’s Temple, which began in 966 BC).

 

2. The Pharaoh of the Exodus: Amenhotep II (Reigned 1453–1419 BC)

Amenhotep II is the most likely candidate for the Pharaoh of the Exodus. He ruled at the approximate time of the biblical event (1446 BC) and exhibited unusual military and political behavior after his fifth year.

Why Amenhotep II Fits the Exodus Account:

  • Sudden Lack of Military Campaigns: After his early campaigns, Amenhotep II stopped major military expeditions, possibly due to the devastating loss of his army in the Red Sea (Exodus 14:28).
  • Religious Upheaval: Amenhotep II promoted a religious shift towards a more monotheistic style of worship, likely reflecting an attempt to counteract the effects of the plagues and the power of Yahweh.
  • Lack of Firstborn Son as Successor: His firstborn son, Webensenu, died young. This aligns with the final plague in Exodus, where all firstborns in Egypt perished (Exodus 12:29).

Webensenu: The Firstborn Who Died?

  • Webensenu was buried in KV35 (Valley of the Kings). His premature death supports the idea that he may have been affected by the tenth plague.
  • The timing of his death, within Amenhotep II’s reign, coincides with the estimated date of the Exodus.

 

3. Archaeological and Chronological Evidence

The 1446 BC Date for the Exodus

Several biblical and archaeological factors support an Exodus in 1446 BC:

  • Biblical Chronology: 1 Kings 6:1 states that Solomon began building the temple in his fourth year (966 BC). Counting back 480 years places the Exodus at 1446 BC.
  • The Merneptah Stele (c. 1208 BC): This Egyptian inscription, from the reign of Pharaoh Merneptah, mentions "Israel" as an already established people in Canaan, meaning the Exodus had to have occurred long before the late 1200s BC.

The Valley of the Kings and the Egyptian Burial of Pharaohs

  • Many New Kingdom pharaohs, including Amenhotep II and Webensenu, were buried in the Valley of the Kings.
  • KV35: Amenhotep II’s tomb contains multiple bodies, but his own mummy was found relatively undisturbed, suggesting no violent overthrow of his reign.

 

4. King Solomon’s Temple and the Time Between the Exodus

  • Solomon's Temple Construction: Began in 966 BC (1 Kings 6:1).
  • Time Between the Exodus and the Temple: 1446 BC (Exodus) → 966 BC (Temple construction) = 480 years.
  • This aligns perfectly with biblical records and historical estimations.

Conclusion

The best-supported theory suggests:

  1. Thutmose III was the Pharaoh of the oppression, ruling during Moses’ early years.
  2. He died, and Moses returned under the reign of his successor, Amenhotep II.
  3. The Exodus occurred in 1446 BC, devastating Egypt.
  4. Amenhotep II’s firstborn son, Webensenu, may have died in the final plague.
  5. This event aligns with biblical records, archaeological evidence from Egypt, and the timeline leading to Solomon’s Temple in 966 BC.
