Exodus 4: Excuses and God’s Reponses

In Exodus 3–4, God calls Moses at the burning bush to deliver the Israelites from Egyptian slavery. However, Moses repeatedly hesitates and raises objections. Eventually, God's anger is kindled against Moses. Let’s break this down:

 

1. Moses' Excuses and God's Responses (Exodus 3–4)

Moses offers multiple reasons why he believes he is unfit for the task, and each time, God reassures him:

  1. "Who am I?" (Exodus 3:11) – A Question of Identity
    • Moses expresses self-doubt, feeling inadequate to confront Pharaoh.
    • God’s response: “I will be with you” (Exodus 3:12).
    • Lesson: God’s presence, not Moses’ ability, is what matters.
  2. "What if they ask for Your name?" (Exodus 3:13) – A Question of Authority
    • Moses fears the Israelites won’t believe him or recognize God's name.
    • God’s response: “I AM WHO I AM” (Exodus 3:14), revealing His eternal nature.
    • Lesson: God equips His messengers with divine authority.
  3. "What if they don’t believe me?" (Exodus 4:1) – A Question of Credibility
    • Moses doubts his ability to convince the Israelites.
    • God’s response: He gives Moses three miraculous signs (staff into a snake, leprous hand, water to blood).
    • Lesson: God provides evidence of His power.
  4. "I am not eloquent" (Exodus 4:10) – A Question of Ability
    • Moses claims he is "slow of speech and tongue," possibly a speech impediment or lack of confidence.
    • God’s response: “Who made man’s mouth? ... I will help you speak and teach you what to say” (Exodus 4:11–12).
    • Lesson: God can use weaknesses for His glory.
  5. "Please send someone else!" (Exodus 4:13) – A Question of Willingness
    • At this point, Moses is not just hesitant but outright refuses.
    • God’s response: “The anger of the LORD burned against Moses” (Exodus 4:14), but He still accommodates him by allowing Aaron to help.
    • Lesson: Persistent reluctance can provoke God's anger.

 

2. Why Did God's Anger Kindle Against Moses?

God's anger flares in Exodus 4:14 because:

  • Moses had seen the burning bush, heard God's voice, and witnessed miracles, yet still doubted.
  • His hesitation was no longer about fear or inadequacy—it was outright unwillingness.
  • God had already addressed every concern, proving He would be with Moses.
  • Moses’ final request—“Send someone else”—showed resistance to God's will, not just insecurity.

This moment is significant because it shows:

  • God is patient but has limits when we resist His calling.
  • God’s anger does not mean abandonment—instead of rejecting Moses, He provides Aaron as a helper.
  • God’s sovereignty will prevail, whether or not Moses felt ready.

 

3. Theological and Practical Lessons

  1. God’s calling comes with His empowerment – Like Moses, many feel unqualified, but God's presence is enough.
  2. God’s patience has a limit – While He is slow to anger, persistent doubt and resistance can provoke divine frustration.
  3. God accommodates weakness but expects obedience – Aaron was allowed to help, but Moses was still responsible.
  4. Faith is necessary for leadership – Hesitation should lead to dependence on God, not avoidance of His call.

 

Conclusion

Moses' hesitation and God's anger reveal a balance between divine patience and expectation. While God understands our fears, prolonged resistance to His will is displeasing. Yet, in His grace, He still equips us to fulfill our calling. Moses, despite his initial reluctance, went on to be one of the greatest leaders in biblical history, showing that God works through even the most hesitant servants.
