Genesis 40: Joseph’s Interpretations While in Prison

Genesis 40 narrates Joseph's experience in prison, where he interprets the dreams of Pharaoh's chief cupbearer (butler) and chief baker. This chapter continues Joseph's journey of faith, showing God's providence in preparing him for his future leadership in Egypt.

 

Context of Genesis 40

Joseph had been falsely accused by Potiphar’s wife and imprisoned. Even in prison, God granted him favor, and he was placed in charge of the other prisoners. This chapter focuses on two of Pharaoh’s officers—his chief cupbearer and chief baker—who were also imprisoned and had disturbing dreams.

 

Verse-by-Verse Breakdown

Verses 1–4: The Arrival of the Butler and Baker

"Some time later, the cupbearer and the baker of the king of Egypt offended their master, the king of Egypt. Pharaoh was angry with his two officials, the chief cupbearer and the chief baker, and put them in custody in the house of the captain of the guard, in the same prison where Joseph was confined."

·         Pharaoh's cupbearer (also called a "butler") and baker were high-ranking officials responsible for food and drink.

·         They had offended Pharaoh in some way, which led to their imprisonment.

·         The captain of the guard (likely Potiphar) assigned them to Joseph’s care.

Verses 5–8: The Dreams and Their Interpretation

"One night they both dreamed—the cupbearer and the baker of the king of Egypt, who were confined in the prison—each his own dream, and each dream had its own meaning. When Joseph came to them the next morning, he saw that they were dejected. So he asked Pharaoh’s officials who were in custody with him in his master’s house, ‘Why do you look so sad today?’ ‘We both had dreams,’ they answered, ‘but there is no one to interpret them.’ Then Joseph said to them, ‘Do not interpretations belong to God? Tell me your dreams.’"

·         Dreams were often seen as divine messages in the ancient world, and Egyptians placed great importance on them.

·         The prisoners were troubled because there was no one to interpret their dreams.

·         Joseph directs them to God—showing his faith that God alone gives interpretation.

Verses 9–15: The Butler’s Dream and Favorable Interpretation

"So the chief cupbearer told Joseph his dream. He said to him, ‘In my dream I saw a vine in front of me, and on the vine were three branches. As soon as it budded, it blossomed, and its clusters ripened into grapes. Pharaoh’s cup was in my hand, and I took the grapes, squeezed them into Pharaoh’s cup, and put the cup in his hand.’"

Joseph’s interpretation:

·         The three branches represent three days.

·         The cupbearer would be restored to his position and serve Pharaoh again.

Joseph then pleads for help:

"But when all goes well with you, remember me and show me kindness; mention me to Pharaoh and get me out of this prison."

·         Joseph expresses his desire for justice.

·         He was falsely accused and did not belong in prison.

Verses 16–19: The Baker’s Dream and the Tragic Interpretation

"When the chief baker saw that Joseph had given a favorable interpretation, he said to Joseph, ‘I too had a dream: On my head were three baskets of bread. In the top basket were all kinds of baked goods for Pharaoh, but the birds were eating them out of the basket on my head.’"

Joseph’s interpretation:

·         The three baskets represent three days.

·         Instead of restoration, the baker would be executed, and birds would eat his flesh.

Verses 20–23: The Fulfillment of the Dreams

"Now the third day was Pharaoh’s birthday, and he gave a feast for all his officials. He restored the chief cupbearer to his position, so that he once again put the cup into Pharaoh’s hand—but he impaled the chief baker, just as Joseph had said to them in his interpretation. The chief cupbearer, however, did not remember Joseph; he forgot him."

·         Pharaoh’s birthday feast was the setting for both men’s fates.

·         The cupbearer was restored, while the baker was executed.

·         Despite Joseph’s hope, the cupbearer forgot about him.

 

Word Study

1. Butler (Cupbearer) – Hebrew: מַשְׁקֶה (mashqeh)

·         Literally means "one who gives drink".

·         In ancient Egypt, the cupbearer was a trusted official responsible for tasting Pharaoh's wine to protect against poisoning.

·         A position of high honor and close proximity to the king.

2. Baker – Hebrew: אֹפֶה (opheh)

·         Refers to one who bakes.

·         The chief baker would oversee all of Pharaoh’s bread and pastries.

·         His offense may have been related to food poisoning or a failed meal for Pharaoh.

3. Dream – Hebrew: חֲלוֹם (chalom)

·         Dreams were believed to be divine messages.

·         The Egyptians had professional dream interpreters, but they were not in prison.

·         Joseph credits God as the only true interpreter of dreams.

4. Interpretation – Hebrew: פִּתְרוֹן (pithron)

·         Means "solution" or "explanation."

·         The Egyptians sought trained dream interpreters, but Joseph shows that only God gives true interpretations.

5. Prison – Hebrew: בֵּית הַסֹּהַר (beit ha-sohar)

·         Literally, "house of confinement."

·         More than a dungeon—this was likely a royal prison where high-ranking officials were kept.

 

Application and Takeaways

1. Trust in God’s Plan, Even in Waiting

·         Joseph’s story teaches patience. Though forgotten by men, he was remembered by God.

·         When we feel overlooked, God is still working behind the scenes.

2. Give Glory to God in Your Gifts

·         Joseph could have taken credit for his interpretations, but he directed glory to God.

·         When we use our talents, we should acknowledge God as the source.

3. Divine Delays Are Not Denials

·         The cupbearer forgot Joseph, but this delay was part of God’s plan.

·         Sometimes, God allows delays to position us for greater opportunities.

 

Conclusion

Genesis 40 shows Joseph’s faith in God despite hardship. His ability to interpret dreams foreshadows his future rise in Egypt. Though forgotten by the cupbearer, he was never forgotten by God. This chapter reminds us to trust in God’s timing, remain faithful in trials, and always give Him the glory.
