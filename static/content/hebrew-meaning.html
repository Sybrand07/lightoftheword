What Does the Word "Hebrew" Mean in the Bible?

The first occurrence of the word "Hebrew" (עִבְרִי, ‘Ivri) in Scripture is in Genesis 14:13, where Abram (later Abraham) is referred to as a "Hebrew":

"Then one who had escaped came and told Abram the Hebrew (עִבְרִי)." (Genesis 14:13, ESV)

What Does "Hebrew" (עִבְרִי, ‘Ivri) Mean?

The exact meaning of ‘Ivri (Hebrew) is debated, but there are a few strong theories:

1.        "One Who Crosses Over" (From עבר, ‘Avar – To Cross, Pass Over)

o    The root verb ‘Avar (עָבַר) means to cross over or pass through.

o    This could refer to Abraham and his descendants as people who "crossed over" from their homeland in Mesopotamia (Ur of the Chaldeans, Genesis 11:31) into Canaan.

o    It could also symbolize being set apart from other nations.

2.      A Connection to the Eberites (Descendants of Eber – עֵבֶר, ‘Ever)

o    Eber (עֵבֶר) was an ancestor of Abraham (Genesis 10:21-25, Genesis 11:14-17).

o    Some scholars believe the term "Hebrew" originally meant descendant of Eber.

3.      A Social or Ethnic Distinction in Egypt

o    In Genesis 39:14, Potiphar’s wife emphasizes Joseph’s foreign status:

"See, he has brought among us a Hebrew (עִבְרִי) to laugh at us."

o    This suggests "Hebrew" may have been used by Egyptians as a label for foreign Semitic people.

How Is "Hebrew" Used Elsewhere?

·         Genesis 40:15 – Joseph tells Pharaoh’s cupbearer that he was "stolen from the land of the Hebrews."

·         Exodus 1:15-19 – The term "Hebrew women" is used by Pharaoh to describe Israelite women.

·         Jonah 1:9 – Jonah says, "I am a Hebrew, and I fear the LORD."

Conclusion

The term "Hebrew" (עִבְרִי, ‘Ivri) likely means "one who has crossed over", referring to Abraham and his descendants being outsiders in foreign lands. The next occurrence will be in Genesis 39:14, where Potiphar’s wife uses it in a derogatory way to emphasize Joseph’s foreign and outsider status in Egypt.
